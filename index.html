Ray Generation

Ray generation computes the origin and direction of each pixel's viewing ray based on the camera geometry.

Perspective

We need to represent a 3D object or scene with a 2D drawing

Many unconventional approaches such fisheye and peripheral

Linear Perspective

  • How to represent a 3D image with a 2D drawing has been studied for hundreds of years
  • The standard approach is linear perspective
  • Objects project onto an image plane in a way that straight lines in the scene become straight lines in the image.

Parallel Projection

  • The simplest type of projection is parallel projection
  • 3D points are mapped to 2D by moving them along a projection direction until they hit the image plane
  • The view produced is determined by the choice of projection direction and image plane
  • Keeps the parallel lines parallel and preserves the size and shape of planar objects that are parallel to the image plane

Perspective Projection

  • Used to produce natural-looking views
  • Project along lines that pass through a single point, the viewpoint, rather than parallel lines
  • Objects father from the viewpoint naturally become smaller when they are projected

Views

There are 2 different views we can use.

We use orthographic views for parallel projection and perspective projection for perspective views

Orthographic

  • All rays will have the direction -w
  • It is possible for objects to be behind the camera
  • Use pixel's image-plane position as the ray's starting point

Perspective

  • All rays have the same origin, at the viewpoint
  • Image plane is no longer placed positioned at e, but rather some distance d in front of e
  • This is called the image plane distance, or the focal length

Shading

Shading is a method used to create or enhance the illusion of depth in an image by varying the amount of darkness in the image.

Three levels of shading are used here, thus making it appear to be three dimensional

Blinn-Phong Shading

  • Many real surfaces show some degree of shininess, or specular reflections
  • Produce a reflection that is at its brightest when v and l are symmetrically positioned across the surface normal
  • The reflection then decreases smoothly as the vectors move away from a mirror configuration

The Phong Exponent

  • The power, p, controls the apparent shininess of the surface

Ambient Shading

  • Surfaces will no illumination will be rendered black
  • Surfaces are illuminated by "ambient" light that comes equally from everywhere

Gouraud Shading

  • Achieves smooth lighting on low-polygon surfaces without heavy computational requirements of calculating lighting for each pixel.
  • Interpolation method used to produce continuous shading of surfaces represented by polygon meshes

Ray Tracing Program

  • We know how to generate a viewing ray, find closest intersection, and shade resulting intersection
  • These are the parts required for a program that produces shaded images with hidden surfaces removed

Shadows

  • If we are at p on a surface, the point is in shadow if we look in the direction of l and see an object
  • If there are no objects, then the light is not blocked and the point is not shaded

Ideal Specular Reflection

  • Ideal specular reflection is also known as mirror reflection
  • Vector r is found using a variant of the Phong lighting reflection equation
  • There are sign changes because the vector d points towards the surface in this case
